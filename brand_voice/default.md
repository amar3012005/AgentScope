# Default Brand Voice

## Tone and Personality

Write in a professional, clear, and authoritative tone. Be direct without being cold. Aim for the voice of a knowledgeable colleague who respects the reader's time and intelligence.

Avoid hype, empty enthusiasm, and filler language. Every sentence should earn its place. If a paragraph can be a sentence, make it a sentence.

## Voice Principles

**Use active voice.** Say "the system processes requests" not "requests are processed by the system." Passive voice is acceptable when the actor is unknown or genuinely irrelevant.

**Keep sentences under 25 words.** Break long compound sentences into shorter ones. One idea per sentence is the goal.

**Address the reader as "you."** Write to a specific person, not a faceless audience. Say "you can configure the dashboard" not "users can configure the dashboard."

**Stay jargon-free unless the term is domain-specific and necessary.** If a simpler word works, use it. If a technical term is required, use it without apology or explanation — your reader is competent.

## Claims and Evidence

**Back every claim with evidence.** Do not write "industry-leading" or "best-in-class" without data to support it. Replace superlatives with specifics: "reduces processing time by 40%" beats "dramatically faster."

**No superlatives without proof.** Words like "revolutionary," "groundbreaking," and "unparalleled" are banned unless accompanied by verifiable evidence.

**Prefer numbers over adjectives.** "Serves 2,000 enterprises" is stronger than "serves many enterprises."

## Inclusivity and Accessibility

**Use inclusive language.** Avoid gendered pronouns when referring to unknown individuals. Use "they" as a singular pronoun. Avoid idioms that may not translate across cultures.

**Write for a global audience.** Spell out acronyms on first use. Avoid region-specific references unless the content targets a specific market.

**Respect the reader's expertise.** Do not over-explain or condescend. Provide context where needed, but trust your audience.

## Structure and Formatting

Lead with the most important information. Use the inverted pyramid: conclusion first, supporting detail after.

Use bullet points and short paragraphs for scannability. Walls of text signal that the writer has not done the work of organizing their thoughts.

Headings should be descriptive and specific. "How to configure alerts" beats "Configuration."

## What to Avoid

- Buzzwords without substance ("synergy," "leverage," "disrupt")
- Exclamation marks in professional content
- Starting sentences with "It is important to note that"
- Hedging language ("we believe," "it seems") — state facts or qualify with data
- Marketing cliches ("unlock the power of," "take your X to the next level")
